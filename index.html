IV. Benchmark Curation & Methodology

4.1 Dataset Consolidation and Performance Targets

To rigorously evaluate the efficacy of gaze-guided weakly supervised segmentation, we consolidated six established medical image libraries into two distinct clinical benchmarks[cite: 267]. The Colonic Polyp Benchmark aggregates 3,784 endoscopic frames from five authoritative sources, including CVC-ClinicDB and PolypGen. This task is primarily defined by significant morphological variation and low tissue-to-background contrast[cite: 271]. Conversely, the Neurological Neoplasm (Brain Tumor) Benchmark comprises 3,064 axial slices derived from T1-CE-MRI sequences provided by Nanfang Hospital and Tianjin Medical University[cite: 272, 273]. All clinical samples are standardized to a $384 \times 384$ resolution to ensure experimental consistency across training and evaluation phases[cite: 274].

4.2 Perception-Driven Data Acquisition

We established a non-intrusive framework to capture clinician visual attention during routine diagnostic inspections[cite: 278, 279]. This method bypasses the logistical burden of explicit manual pixel-wise labeling by recording expert search patterns passively[cite: 279].

Expert Engagement: Four board-certified medical practitioners participated in the recording process[cite: 280]. To minimize idiosyncratic bias, we utilized a two-stage cross-validation protocol [cite: 301, 303]: each clinician first performed an independent review, followed by a mutual audit to ensure attentional consistency across lesion boundaries[cite: 302, 304].

Technical Specifications: Tracking was facilitated by an HTC VIVE Pro Eye system integrated within a Unity-based VR environment[cite: 306, 307]. A multi-point calibration procedure was performed before each session to maintain sub-pixel localization accuracy[cite: 308].

Recording was conducted at a high-frequency sampling rate of 100 Hz. Each image was presented for 2.0 seconds, yielding approximately 100-200 raw coordinate points per sample, which were then mapped to the standard image plane[cite: 310, 311].
