Gaze-Annotated Benchmarks for Weakly Supervised Medical Image Segmentation

GazeMed is a clinically oriented benchmark that couples expert eye-tracking trajectories with two representative lesion segmentation tasks: colonoscopic polyp analysis and contrast-enhanced brain tumor MRI. The page summarizes benchmark composition, expert gaze acquisition, standardized construction workflow, and direct download access.

Dataset Overview

A unified benchmark spanning endoscopy and contrast-enhanced brain MRI

To support rigorous assessment of gaze-guided weak supervision under heterogeneous clinical conditions, the benchmark organizes six medical imaging sources into two task-centered segmentation settings. These tasks were selected because they are clinically important and because they expose complementary challenges in lesion localization, contour recovery, and appearance variability.

Colonic Polyp Benchmark

White-light endoscopy

3,784 images

This subset merges five established colonoscopy datasets—CVC-ClinicDB, CVC-ColonDB, ETIS-LaribPolypDB, Kvasir, and PolypGen—into a unified endoscopic benchmark. Pronounced cross-patient morphology, subtle lesion contrast, and visually complex mucosal backgrounds make it a demanding testbed for gaze-informed spatial localization under weak supervision.

Five-source aggregation Pixel-level masks Approx. 5% multi-lesion

Brain Tumor Benchmark

T1-weighted contrast-enhanced MRI

3,064 slices

This subset contains axial T1-CE-MRI slices collected through institutional collaboration involving Nanfang Hospital and Tianjin Medical University. It complements the endoscopic task with substantial changes in tumor extent, shape, and anatomical context, providing a strong setting for studying gaze-aware boundary reasoning in medical images.

Institutional cohort Pixel-level masks Less than 1% multi-lesion

Unified preprocessing and supervision setting

For consistent model development, all samples are resized to 384 × 384 before training and evaluation. Pixel-wise expert delineations are used only for quantitative assessment, whereas the learning signal during training is derived from expert eye-tracking recordings rather than dense manual outlines.

Gaze Annotation Protocol

Passive expert gaze collection with built-in quality assurance

Instead of requiring clinicians to draw lesion contours during data acquisition, the annotation framework records visual attention while experts inspect images in a controlled reading environment. This strategy substantially lowers annotation burden while preserving clinically meaningful perceptual cues that can be exploited as weak supervisory signals.

Expert protocol

Four board-certified clinicians participated in the collection process. Two focused on the polyp task and two focused on the brain tumor task, ensuring domain-specific interpretation during image inspection.

Quality control

Each task group first completed independent reading sessions and then partially reviewed counterpart cases. Samples showing unstable fixations, abnormal dispersion, or insufficient lesion coverage were excluded before release.

Acquisition setting

Gaze signals were collected with an HTC VIVE Pro Eye headset in a Unity-based environment using SteamVR. Data were sampled at 100 Hz, and each image remained on screen for 2.0 seconds.

Spatial correspondence and stored gaze format

After acquisition, gaze trajectories are projected back onto the image plane and exported as two-dimensional coordinate sequences aligned with the original medical images. Under the fixed viewing duration, each sample typically yields roughly 100–200 valid gaze points, providing a compact yet clinically informative description of expert visual attention.

Dataset Construction Workflow

Standardized transformation from raw eye movements to training-ready gaze sequences

The construction routine follows a canonical sequence: present the image, confirm eye-tracker readiness, record valid gaze samples over a fixed observation window, map these points onto the standardized spatial canvas, and aggregate all sample-wise trajectories into the final benchmark. This design keeps the acquisition process consistent across tasks and readers.

Step 01

Display and calibration check

Each medical image is presented in the virtual reading environment only after the eye-tracker calibration status is confirmed.

Step 02

Continuous gaze capture

During the 2.0-second inspection period, gaze coordinates are sampled continuously, and only valid points are retained as part of the sample-specific sequence.

Step 03

Canonical spatial mapping

The retained fixation points are projected onto the standardized image resolution so that all training samples share the same spatial reference.

Step 04

Sequence aggregation

Sample-level gaze sequences are stored and then combined into a unified repository, producing a benchmark that is ready for downstream weakly supervised segmentation studies.
